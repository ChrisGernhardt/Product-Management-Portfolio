1. Strategy & Planning

Purpose: Set the long-term direction, business context, and success measures for the product.

üë£ Start here: Use the Lean Canvas and North Star Metric to shape your product‚Äôs direction.
  • Define and communicate the product vision, mission, and principles
  • Establish OKRs (SMART) and a North Star Metric to align and measure success
  • Build and maintain a Now‚ÄìNext‚ÄìLater product roadmap
  • Analyze the market using TAM/SAM/SOM, Porter‚Äôs Five Forces, Lean Canvas
  • Segment users and evaluate willingness to pay
  • Identify user personas and define their Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)
  • Define the revenue model and clarify the market category
  • Map and analyze stakeholders using Power/Interest and create a stakeholder register
  • Document strategic themes and measurable success criteria
  • Validate product‚Äìmarket fit using PMF surveys and early traction indicators
  • Analyze the competitive landscape to define positioning and differentiation
  • Document and revisit strategic assumptions that underpin your product bets
  • Set a regular cadence for vision alignment reviews (e.g., quarterly with OKR review)
Frameworks & Artifacts: Vision, Mission, Product Principles, Roadmap, OKRs, Lean Canvas, Personas, JTBD, Market Fit Analysis, Competitive Landscape Map, Strategic Themes, Stakeholder Map, Strategic Assumptions Log
Tips: Anchor strategy in problems, not ideas. Revisit your North Star Metric in every roadmap and OKR review. Use product principles as filters for what not to build. Validate PMF assumptions continuously. Involve key stakeholders early. Make vision reviews a quarterly habit.

2. Discovery & Validation

Purpose: Understand user problems, validate opportunities, and explore solutions to reduce risk before development.

üë£ Start here: Interview 3 users. Fill a Discovery Table. Identify 1 key assumption to test.
  • Interview users and gather feedback through structured qualitative research
  • Run surveys and analyze usage data to validate insights
  • Create and refine problem and opportunity statements
  • Frame and prioritize assumptions and hypotheses
  • Plan and execute experiments (prototypes, MVPs, A/B tests)
  • Create and analyze user flows, task flows, and wire flows
  • Define product solutions using the Why‚ÄìHow‚ÄìWhat model and Solution Definition Canvas
  • Facilitate ideation sessions with ‚ÄúHow Might We‚Ä¶‚Äù, Brainwriting, Crazy 8s
  • Prioritize opportunities using frameworks (RICE, Effort‚ÄìImpact, MoSCoW, etc.)
  • Choose the right MVP or PoC type based on what needs validating
  • Define and run user tests with clear goals and metrics
  • Connect discovery outputs to strategic goals via a Vision-to-Experiment Tree
  • Track insight signal strength and link findings to personas and journeys
Frameworks & Artifacts: Discovery Table, Value Proposition Canvas, Personas, JTBD, Research Repository, Problem Statements, Opportunity Solution Trees, Ideation Methods, Prioritization Models, MVP & PoC Strategy, Prototyping Strategy, User Flows, User Testing Plan, Learning Cards, Vision-to-Experiment Tree, Triage Grid, Persona Mapping Grid
Tips: Validate that the problem exists and matters. Use triangulation for high-confidence insights. Record interviews consistently. MVPs validate value, PoCs validate feasibility. Add signal strength to findings. Embed continuous discovery habits.

3. Requirements & Planning

Purpose: Define what to build, for whom, and under what constraints by translating validated discovery into clearly scoped, prioritized, and testable product requirements.

üë£ Start here: Draft one user story using Gherkin format. Review it with design or engineering.
  • Translate validated discovery into actionable requirements
  • Write user stories with clear, testable acceptance criteria
  • Break work into small, prioritized increments with edge cases and DoR/DoD
  • Run regular backlog refinement sessions with input from design, engineering, QA
  • Capture both functional and non-functional requirements
  • Document known constraints (technical, legal, compliance, UX)
  • Identify and manage risks (viability, feasibility, usability, scope, dependencies)
  • Create and maintain a Dependency Map
  • Conduct strategic and tactical prioritization
  • Use Vision-to-Experiment Tree for alignment
  • Maintain traceability between persona, journey, opportunity, feature, story
Frameworks & Artifacts: Feature Specs, User Stories, Requirement Statements, DoR/DoD, Risk Logs, NFR Checklist, Constraint Log, Prioritization Models, Dependency Map, Refinement Guidelines, Vision-to-Experiment Tree, Opportunity Solution Tree, Product Stack Overview, Persona Mapping Grid
Tips: Write user stories from the user‚Äôs perspective. Add edge cases and failure conditions. Treat non-functional requirements as first-class citizens. Keep backlog refinement lean. Use visual models for stakeholder alignment. Keep backlog clean and confidence-based.

4. Validation & Testing

Purpose: Ensure features function correctly, solve the intended user problems, and meet quality and usability expectations before launch.

üë£ Start here: Build a basic usability test (5 tasks, 5 users). Focus on where people get stuck.
  • Define test coverage for functional, UX, edge case, and non-functional requirements
  • Execute sprint-level validation: functional, regression, exploratory testing
  • Facilitate User Acceptance Testing (UAT) with scripts and stakeholder sign-off
  • Run usability tests with real users
  • Analyze usability findings and update backlog or acceptance criteria
  • Validate MVPs and UX flows against hypotheses and business goals
  • Ensure analytics are instrumented for post-launch validation
  • Run accessibility tests (WCAG compliance, keyboard nav, screen reader support)
  • Document and triage known limitations, issues, and trade-offs
  • Capture insights using structured formats
  • Use bug severity matrices to prioritize test outcomes
  • Update backlog and roadmap priorities based on test results
Frameworks & Artifacts: Sprint Testing Plan, Exploratory Testing Charter, Test Case Management, UAT Scripts, Usability Testing Plan, Results & Reports, UX Review Checklist, Launch Readiness Checklist, Limitations Log, ATDD artifacts, Prototype Evaluation Tools, Accessibility Checklist, Learning Cards, Bug Severity Matrix
Tips: Validate the right thing before validating the thing right. Test early with prototypes. Use exploratory testing charters. Document insights, not just issues. Treat usability bugs with weight. Accessibility is not optional.

5. Launch & Rollout

Purpose: Coordinate internal and external readiness, ensure smooth deployment, and set the foundation for a successful and recoverable release.

üë£ Start here: Create a mini launch checklist (docs ready, support briefed, metrics defined).
  • Finalize the Go-to-Market (GTM) plan with clear goals, timing, and owner alignment
  • Align all stakeholders: product, engineering, marketing, sales, support, legal, compliance
  • Train internal teams using enablement materials and live demos
  • Create and distribute launch materials: FAQs, battlecards, help docs, blog posts, press kits
  • Execute internal dry runs or pilot launches
  • Confirm technical readiness: environments, instrumentation, feature flags, rollback plans
  • Coordinate communications with a messaging plan
  • Monitor post-launch metrics and usage in real time
  • Establish support readiness and incident response plans
  • Conduct a launch retrospective and log all learnings
Frameworks & Artifacts: GTM Plan, Launch Timeline, Enablement Assets, Launch Checklist, Communications Plan, Demo Plan, Rollout Strategy, Monitoring Plan, Incident Response Plan, Retrospective Template
Tips: A good launch is one where you‚Äôre prepared for problems. Use internal launches to pressure-test. Celebrate learning, not just launch. Track enablement adoption to catch readiness gaps.

6. Feedback & Optimization

Purpose: Measure product performance, gather actionable insights, validate impact, and fuel continuous iteration.

üë£ Start here: Read recent NPS/support tickets. Pick one insight and trace it back to a feature.
  • Monitor product KPIs (AARRR, HEART, North Star Metric) and user behavior
  • Collect and analyze qualitative feedback from support, NPS, surveys, social channels
  • Tag and theme feedback to identify high-impact trends
  • Conduct retrospectives after major launches or iterations
  • Evaluate whether features delivered their intended impact
  • Run internal dogfooding loops to uncover usability or reliability issues
  • Conduct postmortems after incidents or major regressions
  • Feed validated insights into discovery, backlog, and roadmap prioritization
  • Reassess alignment to North Star Metric post-release
  • Share structured feedback summaries with product, design, engineering, and GTM teams
Frameworks & Artifacts: Metrics Dashboards, Feedback Channels, Feedback Repository, Impact Validation Summary, Feedback Prioritization Grid, Retrospective Templates, Incident Postmortem Template, Dogfooding Tracker, Feedback-to-Backlog Pipeline, North Star Alignment Review
Tips: Look for patterns with impact. Success is measurable value. Tag feedback by source and persona. Dogfooding helps, but real users > internal assumptions. Feed learnings back into discovery. Use retros for learning, not blame.

7. Team & Ways of Working

Purpose: Define team roles, collaboration norms, and delivery rhythms to foster healthy, high-performing product teams.

üë£ Start here: Join all team rituals for 2 sprints. Take notes. Suggest 1 improvement.
  • Define roles, responsibilities, and ownership boundaries
  • Document escalation paths for blockers, decisions, and stakeholder misalignment
  • Establish and run agile ceremonies: planning, standups, reviews, retrospectives, refinements
  • Set and maintain an effective meeting cadence
  • Track team health and delivery metrics
  • Run periodic team health check-ins
  • Facilitate knowledge sharing: demos, design reviews, tech talks, brown bags
  • Define async collaboration guidelines and documentation expectations
  • Review meeting rituals quarterly for effectiveness and fatigue
Frameworks & Artifacts: Roles & Responsibilities Matrix, Contact Directory, Escalation Paths, Team Charter, Agile Process Overview, Refinement Guidelines, Tool Stack Inventory, Meeting Cadence Calendar, Team Health Radar, Knowledge Sharing Rituals Calendar, Delivery Dashboards
Tips: A healthy team is more valuable than any one feature. Meetings should have a purpose, owner, and timebox. Culture is how decisions are made when you‚Äôre not in the room. Distributed teams need clarity. Use retrospectives for team health and morale.

8. Compliance & Security

Purpose: Ensure the product meets applicable legal, privacy, regulatory, and security standards throughout the lifecycle, without compromising user trust or product velocity.

üë£ Start here: Ask your engineering lead which frameworks apply (e.g. GDPR, SOC2). Log what data is being stored and where.
  • Identify applicable compliance frameworks (e.g., GDPR)
  • Engage legal, and data privacy early in product planning and refinement
  • Document compliance and data handling requirements for new features
  • Perform security threat modeling and risk assessments during planning
  • Follow secure development practices and maintain a Secure Dev Checklist
  • Ensure privacy by design: data minimization, user control, transparency
  • Include legal and compliance sign-off in pre-launch checklists
  • Maintain audit logs of product changes related to sensitive data or systems
  • Coordinate incident response plans with cross-functional teams
  • Stay updated on regulatory changes and emerging obligations
Frameworks & Artifacts: Compliance Requirements Matrix, Secure Development Checklist, Privacy by Design Guidelines, Data Handling Documentation, Security Risk Register, Legal & Infosec Review Checklist, Launch Sign-Off Checklist, Audit Trail Documentation, Incident Response Plan, Compliance Stakeholder Map
Tips: Involve legal early. Security and compliance are continuous. Document risk decisions. Build privacy features as product features. Don‚Äôt wait until a breach or audit to create your security documentation.
